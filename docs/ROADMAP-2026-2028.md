## 0. Executive summary & two-year North Star

Ephemerent builds intelligence that emerges from many small, temporary minds. The wedge is one sentence: **watch a dozen agents work your codebase in plain sight, entirely on your machine.** Today that means Orrery — a Live, local-first agentic code editor that splits a goal into tasks, runs each in its own git worktree, takes best-of-N attempts, auto-judges the winner, and merges. Parallel agents, visible and steerable, on hardware you own.

### The position no funded competitor can claim at once

Four properties, held together:

| Property | What it means | Why incumbents can't match it |
|---|---|---|
| **Legibility** | Diffs, plans, and tool-calls side-by-side; redirectable mid-flight | Devin parallelizes in invisible cloud sandboxes; Cursor/Zed bury parallel work in panels |
| **Locality** | Runs on your machine; Ollama local is free | Cloud-coupled products are structurally tethered to their backend |
| **Independence** | Model-agnostic — Ollama, OpenAI, Gemini, DeepSeek, Anthropic; BYO-key | Frontier-lab tools steer you toward one model |
| **Verification** | Best-of-N judged by execution, not vibes | The hard problem most teams skip — only ~52% of production teams have evals |

Each property alone exists somewhere. The four together do not. A cloud-coupled leader cannot credibly claim locality or independence without unwinding its business model. That is the moat: not a feature, a structural contradiction in everyone else.

### The narrative spine

**"LLMs now, layers later."** We ship LLM-powered legible coding agents today and research the layers that integrate alongside them — never replacing them — tomorrow. The two-year arc runs from *parallel LLM agents on local fleets* toward *world-model-grounded, verified orchestration over those same fleets*. Each product maps to a research direction so the agenda reads as one argument, not a catalog:

- **Orrery** (Live) → R1 emergent orchestration
- **Vellum** (Preview) → R2/R5 verifiable selection, spatial agents
- **Arbiter** (Research, elevated) → R2 verifiable selection — the fundable hard problem
- **Seed** (Research) → R3 latent world models for code
- **Colony** (Preview) → R7 distributed compute mesh

The economic case is forcing this. Token costs fell ~40x, but agent costs *rose* ~4x through loops and retries. Efficient, verified orchestration is no longer a nicety; it is the only way the unit economics of agentic coding close.

### Where the lab is today

One Live product (Orrery, Windows). One founder. Bootstrapped. Live at ephemerent.com. A public pledge already on the record: **≥10% of profits** to open research and real problems — disease, poverty, food and water, energy — plus community and anti-job-loss policy research. A floor, not a ceiling. The honest gaps are equally on the record: a `pk_test_` Stripe key on a live paid site with no webhook (paid users never upgraded), proof-thin product pages, and Vellum's "proof" still theatrical rather than literal.

### The three strategic bets for 2026–2028

These are bets, marked as bets — forward-looking work we will execute against, not facts.

1. **Emergent multi-agent coordination is the lab's distinctive claim.** Today's protocols (A2A v1.0, MCP) assume static roles and pairwise hand-offs and explicitly lack frameworks for spontaneous collaboration and distributed consensus. Gossip-protocol-style decentralized coordination is a named open gap — the natural home for an emergence lab.

2. **Verification is the most fundable problem of the next two years.** As capability saturates (Opus-class ~88% SWE-bench Verified), value migrates to trust. With a ~37% lab-to-production gap and 50x cost variance for equal accuracy, "verification infrastructure for emergent multi-agent output" is the strongest narrative we can carry to non-dilutive funders. Arbiter and Vellum are its product expression.

3. **World models become a planning substrate for code.** 2026 mainstreamed world models (Genie 3, World Labs/Marble, LeCun's AMI). For code, that means agents that simulate execution and repo state before acting — a planning *and* verification primitive. Seed is the bet.

Funding follows the bets, not equity: NAIRR / NSF ACCESS (window ~Jun 15–Jul 31) and LTFF/SFF now, stacked cloud credits (Google/AWS/Azure, up to ~$350K/$100K/$150K), revenue-based financing deferred until ARR is predictable.

## 1. Research agenda 2026–2028

Ephemerent is a research lab that ships a product. The discipline that keeps those two halves honest is one line: **LLMs now, layers later.** We ship LLM-powered, legible, verifiable coding agents today. We research world-model-grounded planning and verification to integrate *alongside* LLMs tomorrow — never as a replacement, always as a measured addition with an eval that proves it earned its place.

Our seven research directions (R1–R7) are not seven bets. They are three fundable frontiers, each with a product that expresses it and a public artifact that proves it. The market is moving where the thesis already pointed. This section states what we will actually do over eight quarters, and what gets integrated alongside the LLM, and when.

The three frontiers:

- **Frontier A — Emergent multi-agent coordination** (R1 emergent orchestration, R7 distributed compute mesh). Product: **Colony**. Spine claim: emergence is the lab's name and its strongest distinctive position.
- **Frontier B — Verification and evaluation as the hard problem** (R2 verifiable selection). Products: **Arbiter** (the verification layer, strategically the most important) and **Vellum** (spatial verification). This is the most fundable two-year narrative.
- **Frontier C — World models as a planning substrate** (R3 latent world models, R4 multimodal latent reasoning, R5 spatial/embodied agents, R6 wave compute substrates). Product: **Seed**.

### Frontier A — Emergent multi-agent coordination

**The open gap.** Today's interoperability protocols assume the problem is already solved. A2A v1.0 and MCP both encode *static roles* and *pairwise hand-offs*. Neither has a framework for spontaneous collaboration, distributed consensus, or emergent task-sharing. Gossip-protocol-style decentralized coordination is a named open gap. For a lab called Ephemerent — "intelligence that emerges from many small, temporary minds" — this is the natural home and the most distinctive claim we can stake.

**What we will do (8 quarters).** Orrery already runs best-of-N agents in parallel git worktrees with an auto-judge. That is coordination with a *central* dispatcher. The research question is what coordination looks like when there is no dispatcher — when agents share partial results, vote, and re-allocate work via gossip rather than a fixed plan. We will (1) instrument Orrery's existing multi-agent runs to log every task split, attempt, and merge as a coordination trace; (2) prototype a gossip-style consensus layer where worktree agents exchange intermediate diffs and converge without a central judge; (3) scale that layer to **Colony** — federated volunteer GPUs, async gradient averaging, credit-backed rewards — so coordination is tested at fleet scale, not just on one laptop.

**Integration point (alongside LLMs).** The LLM remains the per-agent reasoner. The emergent layer is the *protocol between* agents — it schedules and arbitrates, it does not generate code. Target integration: gossip-consensus task allocation behind a flag in Orrery by **Q4 2027**, default-eligible once it beats central dispatch on the coordination benchmark below.

**Credible output.** An open **multi-agent coordination dataset** (anonymized Orrery traces: splits, attempts, judge decisions, merges) and a tech report on gossip vs. central dispatch, published when Colony reaches a measurable contributor mesh.

### Frontier B — Verification and evaluation as the hard problem

**The open gap.** Capability is saturating. Opus-class models reach ~88% on SWE-bench Verified, so value migrates from *generating* answers to *trusting* them. Yet only ~52% of production teams run evals at all; the lab-to-production gap runs ~37%; and there is a documented 50× cost variance for equal accuracy. The weakest-covered cases are exactly ours: multi-agent, long-horizon, self-improving. "Verification infrastructure for emergent multi-agent output" is therefore the most fundable narrative we have, and the one most aligned with the actual bottleneck in the field. Token costs fell ~40× while agent costs rose ~4× through loops and retries — efficient, *verified* orchestration is now an economic necessity, not a nicety.

**What we will do (8 quarters).** **Arbiter** is the through-line and we are elevating it. We will (1) ship execution-based scoring as the default judge in Orrery — winners chosen by tests passed and behavior observed, not by an LLM eyeballing a diff; (2) build a public **eval harness** with both *step-level* scoring (was each task split, tool-call, and hand-off sound?) and *outcome-level* scoring (did the merged result work?), because step+outcome eval for emergent multi-agent output is our strongest two-year story; (3) stand up a **drift leaderboard** that re-measures solve-rate as cloud models change underneath us, so customers see when a model upgrade silently regresses their workflows. **Vellum** is the spatial expression: we make "prove, don't eyeball" *literally* true by replacing theatrical setTimeout proofs with a real WASM software rasterizer / WebGPU compute path — verification with no GPU in the loop, doubling as a live demo of the lab's verification research.

**Integration point.** Execution-based scoring replaces LLM-judge as Orrery's default selector by **Q2 2027**. The drift leaderboard goes public the same quarter. Vellum's real rasterizer ships by **Q3 2027**.

**Credible output.** A **public eval harness** (step + outcome) plus a **multi-agent verification benchmark** anyone can run, and a short paper on execution-based selection vs. LLM-judge cost/accuracy.

### Frontier C — World models as a planning substrate

**The open gap.** 2026 mainstreamed world models — Genie 3, World Labs/Marble, LeCun's AMI direction. For code, the analogue is an agent that *simulates execution and repo state before acting*. That is simultaneously a planning primitive and a verification primitive: predict the consequence of a patch, prune the bad branches before spending tokens on them.

**What we will do (8 quarters).** **Seed** is the bet, and we mark it as a bet. We will (1) collect patch-consequence data from Orrery runs (proposed diff → test/build outcome); (2) train a small predictor that estimates the outcome of a patch *before* execution and use it to prune best-of-N candidates; (3) explore latent and multimodal reasoning (R3/R4) and spatial/embodied agents (R5) where Vellum's geometry gives a grounded signal. R6 — wave/RF/photonic analog compute substrates — is our longest-horizon bet and stays explicitly research-only across this window.

**Integration point.** Seed runs *advisory only* first — it ranks candidates, the LLM and Arbiter still decide. A pre-execution patch predictor pruning best-of-N lands behind a flag by **Q1 2028**, integrated alongside (never replacing) the LLM agents, and only promoted if it improves solve-rate-per-token on Arbiter's harness.

**Credible output.** An open **patch-consequence dataset** and a tech report on pre-execution pruning: how much compute a software world model saves versus blind best-of-N.

### What is shipped versus what is a bet

To stay on the record: execution-based verification, the eval harness, the drift leaderboard, and Vellum's real rasterizer are **commitments** — they are extensions of work already running in Orrery and Vellum, and they fund themselves by making the product more trustworthy. Gossip-style emergent coordination, Colony at fleet scale, Seed's software world model, and every line of R6 are **bets** — flagged, advisory-first, and promoted only when an Arbiter eval proves they beat the LLM-only baseline on solve-rate-per-token. Nothing replaces the LLM. Each layer is added when, and only when, it is measured to earn its place. That is the whole discipline: layers later, but only the layers that pass the test.

## 3. Go-to-market, pricing & community

The roadmap above assumes users arrive and convert. This section is how. We are not a sales-led company and will not pretend to be one. Ephemerent has one founder, no quota-carrying reps, and no budget to buy attention. The motion that fits is **product-led plus community-led**: the free local tier of Orrery does the selling, and a public community does the supporting, the convincing, and the recruiting of the next user. Everything in this section is built to make those two engines turn without a sales team in the loop. Pricing tiers ($40/$100/$200), Discord, and Ollama/DeepSeek support are all confirmed Live.

### 3.1 The motion: product-led, community-led

The wedge is Orrery's free local tier. Ollama-backed local inference is genuinely free to the user and costs us nothing per token, so we can let people run real workloads — multiple agents, real worktrees, real merges — before they ever see a pricing page. The job of the funnel is to get a developer from download to a merged best-of-N result fast, then let the product's own usage limits surface the upgrade. Community is the second engine: a place where the founder is visibly present, where early users answer each other, and where the most engaged become ambassadors who carry the launch further than paid channels could.

### 3.2 Discord as the day-one hub

Discord stands up **on or before launch day**, not after. It is the single hub for three jobs that would otherwise need three tools: support (a triage channel the founder reads daily), feedback (a channel that feeds directly into the roadmap), and ambassadors (a recognized role for users who help others and file good bugs). This is deliberate: support load and feedback signal are the same conversations, and keeping them in one public room means every answer is searchable by the next person. The bet is that a small, high-context community of local-first developers compounds faster than a support inbox — and that the founder being on the record in that room is itself a differentiator against cloud-coupled incumbents.

### 3.3 Launch sequencing

We launch where developers actually evaluate tools: **Hacker News (Show HN) and StackShare**, not Product Hunt. Dev tools structurally underperform on Product Hunt's broad-consumer audience; HN and StackShare select for the local-first, model-agnostic buyer we want. The honest positioning carries the post — "parallel agents in plain sight, on your own machine, with the model you choose" — three claims cloud-coupled leaders cannot make together.

A front-page HN spike is a load event, and our architecture turns it into an advantage. Because the entry point is the **free local tier**, a 10x or 50x traffic spike does not hit our inference bill — those users run on their own hardware. The funnel for a spike is: HN front page → download page → free local install → Discord. Concretely, before we post we will have: the download page served from static/CDN hosting; the Discord invite live and moderated; the Stripe webhook and entitlement path working end-to-end (see 3.6) so that anyone who *does* convert during the spike actually gets upgraded; and a status/known-issues pinned message ready. We do not pay for cloud tokens during our own launch, which is exactly the kind of cost discipline the thesis demands.

### 3.4 Activation instrumentation

Most PLG funnels lose 40–60% of users before activation and never measure it. We will measure it from day one. The target is **time-to-first-value under 10 minutes** — download to first merged best-of-N result — and an **activation rate of 40–60%** of installs reaching that moment.

The funnel we instrument:

| Stage | Definition | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Visit → Download | HN/StackShare visitor starts the installer | 8–12% |
| Download → Install | Installer completes, app opens | 70%+ |
| Install → First run | User defines a goal and starts agents | 60%+ |
| First run → **Activation** | First merged best-of-N result (first value) | **40–60%** |
| Activation → Habit | 3+ goals run within 7 days | 25–35% |
| Habit → Paywall hit | Trips a usage milestone (see 3.5) | 15–25% |
| Paywall → Paid | Converts to Pro/Max/Ultra | 3–6% |
| Time-to-first-value | Median download → first merge | **< 10 min** |

These targets are bets, not guarantees; the first 90 days exist to replace them with measured numbers. The non-negotiable is that every stage is instrumented before launch, because a funnel we cannot see is a funnel we cannot fix.

### 3.5 Reverse trial and usage-milestone paywall

New users get **full features immediately**, then **downgrade to free** rather than hitting an expiry wall — a reverse trial. This matters for a local-first tool: we want people to experience parallel best-of-N with cloud models and proper quotas, feel the value, and *keep a working product* when the trial ends instead of being locked out. The free tier remains genuinely useful (local models, core editor, worktree workflow), so a lapsed trial is a downgrade, not a shutdown.

The paywall is **usage-milestone-based, not calendar-based**. Instead of "your 14 days are up," the prompt appears when the user trips a real limit — cloud token quota exhausted, or N concurrent parallel attempts exceeded, or M goals in a period. Tying the ask to the moment of demonstrated value is the honest version of a paywall: you are asked to pay precisely when you have proven you use the thing.

### 3.6 Pricing-page redesign

The current page is a flat list with a `pk_test_` key and no webhook — meaning **paid users were never actually upgraded**. That is the first thing to fix, and it gates everything else in this section. The redesign:

- **A value headline, not the word "Pricing"** — lead with what the tiers buy ("Run more agents in parallel"), not the act of charging.
- **Annual default**, with the **dollar savings shown** next to each tier, not a vague "save 20%."
- **Three visible tiers** — Free / Pro $40 / Max $100 — with **Max marked "Most Popular,"** and **Ultra $200 kept visible as the anchor**. The $200 tier exists to make $100 read as reasonable; it is removed at our peril.
- **No-credit-card CTA** to start the reverse trial, with **Stripe Link + Apple Pay / Google Pay** to make the eventual conversion one tap.
- **FAQ placed above the final CTA**, trust badges near the cards, so the last objection is answered before the last button.
- **Transparent BYO-key local pricing** stated plainly. Showing that local inference is free and BYO-key is honest billing is itself the differentiator against opaque per-token competitors.

### 3.7 Migration to flat-plan-plus-credit-pool

Flat pricing breaks under agentic loops — token costs fell ~40x while agent costs rose ~4x through retries and best-of-N fan-out, so a single power user on a flat plan can erase the margin of ten. The field converged on the fix in 2025–26, and we follow it: **each paid tier becomes a flat plan plus a monthly credit pool** of bundled DeepSeek tokens, with BYO-key always available and always free of our markup. The three tiers and the $200 anchor stay visible; what changes is that heavy cloud usage draws down a pool the user can see and top up, instead of silently burning our margin. This keeps the pricing honest in both directions: predictable for normal users, fairly metered for the heavy ones, and never a black box.

### 3.8 Content and SEO engine

Our cheapest durable channel is writing the things only this lab can write. Three streams, build-in-public throughout:

1. **Research-agenda posts** — R1–R7 explained plainly, each tied to a product, so the agenda reads as one coherent argument rather than seven loose bets.
2. **Benchmark write-ups** — real solve-rate numbers from Arbiter's eval leaderboard as models drift, labeled honestly (real vs. simulated), turning verification from a claim into published evidence.
3. **Build-in-public** — shipping notes, funnel numbers we are comfortable sharing, and the honest version of what is Live versus what is a bet.

The compounding asset is credibility: a verification lab that publishes its own benchmarks, on the record, is the SEO moat no competitor can copy without doing the same hard work.

## 4. Funding, runway & sustainability

That community-and-product motion keeps acquisition cheap; this section keeps the lights on without selling the company. The lab is bootstrapped and intends to stay that way. We are not raising a priced equity round, not selling board seats, and not taking strategic investment that comes with control or roadmap influence. The capital plan below is non-dilutive first by design: equity-free compute, research and philanthropic grants, and product revenue — in that order — with revenue-based financing held in reserve until it is genuinely cheap. The goal is to fund two years of research-grade work without surrendering the independence that is itself part of the thesis.

### 4.1 The capital stack, in priority order

**Tier 1 — Equity-free compute (apply now).** Compute is our largest cost driver and the one most easily covered without giving up anything. Three sources stack:

- **NAIRR** — free allocations of H100/A100 capacity for AI research. This is the single best fit: it is research-gated, costs zero dollars and zero equity, and directly underwrites Seed (world-model training) and Arbiter (eval runs at scale).
- **NSF ACCESS** — academic/independent compute allocations. The submission window runs **~Jun 15–Jul 31**; this is the nearest hard deadline on the calendar and should be treated as a sprint, not a someday.
- **Startup cloud credits** — Google (~$350K), Azure (~$150K), AWS (~$100K), stackable across providers. These cover inference burst, CI, and Vellum's rasterizer/WebGPU build-out. Apply to all three; they are not mutually exclusive.

**Tier 2 — Research & philanthropic grants (apply Q3 2026 onward).** Our 10%-pledge and verification/safety agenda are not garnish here — they are the exact thing these funders exist to back.

| Funder | Size | Cadence | What they want | Our edge |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SFF | ~$20–40M round | Periodic rounds | High-impact AI safety / x-risk-relevant work | Verification-as-safety; emergent multi-agent coordination is an open, fundable gap |
| LTFF | Rolling | Continuous, independent-friendly | Long-termist research, small independent teams | Single founder, bootstrapped, on-record pledge — exactly their profile |
| FLI | Up to $4M | Program-dependent | AI safety, governance, anti-job-loss | Pledge explicitly funds anti-job-loss policy research |
| Mozilla Open Source AI Awards | ~$50K | Annual, **~mid-March** | Open-source, public-interest AI | Open research output + image-free, legible, local-first stack |

The through-line for every grant application is the same and it is true: **verification is the hard, fundable problem.** Capability is saturating (Opus-class ~88% on SWE-bench Verified); only ~52% of production teams have evals; there is a ~37% lab-to-production gap and 50x cost variance for equal accuracy. "Verification infrastructure for emergent multi-agent output" is our most fundable two-year narrative, and Arbiter/Vellum are its product expression. The 10%-pledge ("≥10% of profits to open research and real problems — a floor, not a ceiling") is what makes the philanthropic framing honest rather than opportunistic.

**Tier 3 — Revenue-based financing (deferred; bet, not plan).** Pipe / Capchase advance against predictable recurring revenue. We deliberately do **not** pursue these now: with ARR still forming, the implied cost is high and the terms are worst when you need the money most. The bet is to revisit RBF only once monthly recurring revenue is stable and forecastable — likely not before late 2027 — and only to smooth working capital, never to fund research. RBF is non-dilutive but it is debt; it earns its place only when it is cheap.

### 4.2 Application sequence and timing

| Window | Action | Why now |
|---|---|---|
| **Now (Jun 2026)** | File NSF ACCESS before **Jul 31**; submit NAIRR; open Google/AWS/Azure credit applications | ACCESS window is open and closes ~Jul 31; compute is the cheapest win |
| **Now (Jun 2026)** | Submit LTFF (rolling) | No deadline cost to applying early; independent-friendly |
| **Q3 2026** | Prepare SFF materials; ship Stripe webhook + entitlements so revenue is real | SFF rounds are periodic; verification narrative needs a working product behind it |
| **~Mar 2027** | Mozilla Open Source AI Awards (~mid-March) | Annual; aligns with open-research deliverables maturing |
| **Late 2027 (bet)** | Evaluate Pipe/Capchase | Only once ARR is predictable |

Sequencing logic: **lock compute first** (zero-cost, near-term deadline), **layer grants second** (slower, narrative-dependent, fund the research the product cannot yet pay for), **keep debt last** (cheapest only when least needed).

### 4.3 Runway logic

Runway rests on a structural advantage in our cost model: **BYO-key + local-first means inference COGS is largely the user's, not ours.**

- **Revenue.** Hybrid pricing — three visible tiers (Pro $40 / Max $100 / Ultra $200) plus a credit pool — with the $200 Ultra as the anchor. Ollama-local usage is free to the user and free to us. Cloud usage runs on the user's own API key or on bundled DeepSeek quotas we buy at wholesale.
- **COGS offset.** The credit pool is sized so that credits sold cover the inference we actually pay for, with a margin. Because BYO-key shifts most token cost to the user, our COGS scales far slower than seats. This is the inverse of the field's pain point: token prices fell ~40x but agent costs rose ~4x via loops and retries; flat-priced competitors absorb that, we do not.
- **The equation.** Months of runway = (cash on hand + grants landed) ÷ (fixed burn − net contribution from Pro/Max/Ultra after credit-covered COGS). Equity-free compute drives the largest line in fixed burn toward zero. Grants extend the numerator without touching the cap table. Revenue is the only piece we control directly, so activation discipline (<10 min time-to-first-value, 40–60% activation) is a funding lever, not just a growth metric.

### 4.4 What we are explicitly not doing

We are not trading equity or control for runway. No priced round, no board seats, no strategic investor steering the roadmap, no acquisition-bait posture. Every instrument above is either a grant (no claim on the company), free compute (no claim on the company), or, eventually, non-dilutive debt against revenue we already earn. Independence is not a vanity position — it is what lets us say "LLMs now, layers later" and mean it, and what lets the pledge be a floor rather than a marketing line. If the choice is ever between faster and independent, the roadmap defaults to independent.

## 5. Website & brand technical roadmap

The funding plan keeps the lab independent; the website is where that independence is argued in public. The website is the lab on the record. Today its strongest asset is copy and positioning; its weakest is execution underneath that copy. The audit is unambiguous: three disconnected design systems with name-colliding tokens, ~250 lines of duplicated Orrery↔Vellum inline CSS, render-blocking `@import` fonts, an SVG `og:image` that renders blank social previews, no JSON-LD, a `pk_test_` Stripe key on a live paid site with no webhook (so paid users never actually upgraded), WCAG contrast failures on `--faint` labels, an O(n²) unguarded `rAF` loop in `ephemerent.js`, stored-XSS via unescaped `display_name`, and product pages that are proof-thin. The site reads like a serious lab and runs like a prototype. This roadmap closes that gap in three phases.

The sequencing principle: **correctness and revenue before craft, craft before spectacle.** We do not ship scroll-driven variable-weight typography on top of a broken Stripe webhook. Every visual technique below is progressive enhancement — feature-detected, `prefers-reduced-motion` gated, with a working baseline when the enhancement is unavailable. Nothing degrades to broken.

### Phase 1 — Foundation (ship first)

**Goal.** Make the site correct, secure, billable, and accessible. This is the phase where bugs that cost money or leak user data get fixed. No new visual surface area.

**Key work.**
- **One shared design-token root.** Build on the `orrery.css` token layer (already the mature, properly-tokenized base). Editorial, product, and Vellum themes become override layers, not parallel systems. This single change unblocks every later phase — there is no fluid type scale or View Transition worth building on three colliding token sets.
- **Stripe webhook + live key.** Replace `pk_test_` with the live publishable key, stand up the webhook endpoint, and wire automatic entitlement on `checkout.session.completed` / `customer.subscription.updated`. This is a revenue bug, not a feature: paid users are currently charged-adjacent and never upgraded. Highest dollar-value item in the entire roadmap. (Same Q3 2026 webhook the product and funding sections gate everything on.)
- **Escape user strings.** Fix the stored-XSS by escaping `display_name` (and every other user-controlled field) on render. Harden auth surfaces.
- **Guard/cap `ephemerent.js`.** Replace the O(n²) unguarded `rAF` with an IntersectionObserver gate, a `prefers-reduced-motion` short-circuit, and a hard node cap.
- **Fonts off the critical path.** Replace render-blocking `@import` with `preconnect` + `<link rel="preload">`; evaluate self-hosting with `font-display: swap` and `size-adjust` to kill CLS.
- **Social + structured data.** Ship a real 1200×630 PNG `og:image` (the SVG currently renders blank in every scraper) and JSON-LD (`Organization` + `SoftwareApplication` + `WebSite`).
- **Accessibility floor.** Fix `--faint` contrast failures (APCA pass on warm accents), add `:focus-visible` rings, skip links, and a `<noscript>` fallback.

**Risk / fallback.** Lowest-risk phase — most items are corrections, not additions. The token consolidation is the one item that can regress visual output; mitigate with a before/after visual-diff pass per page before merge. Stripe goes to a test-mode dry run against a sandbox event before the live key flips.

**Definition of done.** Live Stripe key with a verified webhook completing an end-to-end test purchase → entitlement. Zero XSS on the `display_name` vector (manual injection test passes). Lighthouse: Performance ≥ 90, Accessibility ≥ 95, Best Practices ≥ 95, SEO = 100. CWV in the green: LCP < 2.5s, CLS < 0.1, INP < 200ms. Rich-results test passes for all three JSON-LD types; a non-blank social preview renders in the Twitter/Discord/Slack scrapers.

### Phase 2 — Editorial craft + motion

**Goal.** Earn the "serious lab" read at the typographic and motion level. This is where the site stops looking hand-written and starts looking authored. Everything here sits on the Phase 1 token root.

**Key work.**
- **Optical sizing + fluid modular scale.** `font-optical-sizing: auto` with explicit `opsz`, and a `clamp()`-based modular type scale so headings and body track viewport without breakpoint cliffs.
- **Print-grade typography.** `text-wrap: balance` on headings, `text-wrap: pretty` on body, `hanging-punctuation`, and `initial-letter` drop caps on section leads. This is the cheapest credibility-per-byte in the roadmap.
- **Cross-document View Transitions.** Shared-element morph between index → product pages (the product card morphs into the hero). Behind `@view-transition` — browsers without support get an instant, correct navigation.
- **Scroll-driven reveals + variable-weight-on-scroll.** CSS `view()`/`scroll()` driven reveals with a JS IntersectionObserver fallback, and a variable-font weight axis bound to scroll position. All of it `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` → static.
- **Animated grain.** Subtle animated SVG `feTurbulence` film grain, capped and reduced-motion gated.
- **Subgrid / `@container` hairline bento.** A `subgrid` + container-query hairline bento grid for the R1–R7 research rows and the Work rows — alignment that holds across viewport without magic numbers.
- **Extract duplicated CSS.** Pull the ~250 lines of duplicated Orrery↔Vellum inline CSS into a shared product stylesheet on the Phase 1 tokens.

**Key techniques.** `@view-transition` + `view-transition-name`; `view()`/`scroll()` timelines; variable-font axis bound to scroll; `subgrid` + `@container`; `text-wrap` + `hanging-punctuation` + `initial-letter`; Lenis (~3KB) smooth scroll, disabled on touch and under reduced-motion.

**Risk / fallback.** This is the phase where motion can hurt — jank, motion sickness, layout shift. Every effect is feature-detected and reduced-motion gated; the baseline (Phase 1) is the guaranteed-correct fallback, so any failed enhancement degrades to a clean static page. Watch INP on the scroll-bound weight axis — if it regresses, drop to discrete weight steps. Lenis is the only added JS dependency; keep it deferred and off-thread where possible.

**Definition of done.** No CWV regression from Phase 1 (LCP, CLS, INP all hold). View Transitions verified working in a Chromium build and verified gracefully absent (no broken nav) in a non-supporting build. Full keyboard pass clean with visible focus throughout. Reduced-motion request produces a fully static, fully legible page with zero animation. Subjective bar: the typography passes an Awwwards-level review — balanced headings, no orphans, no widows, optical alignment.

### Phase 3 — Proof + WebGL

**Goal.** Replace claims with evidence and theatrics with truth. Phase 3 makes the product pages convert and makes Vellum's "no GPU in the loop" proof literally true rather than `setTimeout`-driven.

**Key work.**
- **OGL GPU line rendering for V3 wireframes.** OGL (~29KB) instanced GPU lines behind a WebGL2 feature-detect, with a 2D canvas fallback. Distance-to-plane volumetric glow in a fragment shader.
- **Vellum WASM software rasterizer.** Make "prove, don't eyeball" literally true: a WASM software rasterizer running in a Worker on `OffscreenCanvas`, so Vellum's verification renders an actual frame with no GPU in the loop. This doubles as the lab's verification-research demo — the strategically load-bearing piece. (Same rasterizer the Q2/Q3 2027 product and research milestones depend on.)
- **Real product proof.** Replace proof-thin pages with screenshots, video (clearly labeled real vs. simulated), and real benchmarks. No more theatrical proof anywhere on the site.
- **Conversion infrastructure.** Hero CTA buttons, a comparison table (vs. cloud-coupled leaders on locality + model-agnosticism + observable parallelism — the three claims they structurally cannot make together), a security section, an FAQ, and the pricing-page redesign: three tiers, annual default with dollar savings shown, anchor-hero-decoy with the $200 Ultra as anchor, no-credit-card trial CTA, Stripe Link + Apple/Google Pay, FAQ above the final CTA, trust badges near the cards.
- **Research-agenda page.** Expand R1–R7 into a standalone page, each direction tied to its product expression (Arbiter→R2, Seed→R3, Vellum→R5, Colony→R7), so the agenda reads as one coherent bet rather than a list.

**Key techniques.** OGL instanced lines + WebGL2 detect + 2D fallback; distance-to-plane volumetric glow in fragment shader; WASM + `OffscreenCanvas` software rasterizer in a Worker; optionally WebGPU compute particles (feature-detected) with a WebGL2 transform-feedback fallback; poster-first lazy-hydrated 3D embeds.

**Risk / fallback.** Highest-effort, highest-GPU-risk phase. Every WebGL/WASM surface is poster-first: a static image or pre-rendered frame ships immediately and hydrates only on view, only when the feature is detected. No GPU → 2D fallback. No WASM → keep the current visual but label it honestly as a simulation rather than claiming verified proof. The conversion infra (CTAs, comparison table, FAQ, pricing) carries no technical risk and should land even if a WebGL surface slips — these are the items that actually move activation and revenue.

**Definition of done.** Vellum renders at least one verification frame through the real WASM rasterizer with no GPU in the loop, and the page no longer makes a proof claim it cannot back. WebGL surfaces hold 60fps on mid-tier hardware and fall back cleanly to 2D with no broken state. Pricing page is live with the redesigned three-tier layout and the activation instrumentation wired (target <10 min time-to-first-value, 40–60% activation measured, not assumed). No CWV regression. Subjective bar: a first-time visitor can, within one scroll of a product page, see real proof and reach a CTA — the page sells without overclaiming.
